What a newcomer needs to understand (before how it works)

For a cryptographer (or serious engineer), understanding proceeds in this order:

  1. What problem class does this solve?
  2. What guarantees does it claim?
  3. What does it explicitly not claim?
  4. What does correct usage look like?
  5. What does misuse look like?
  6. Only then: how is it implemented?

Your primer partially answers (4), weakly touches (2), and is silent on (1), (3), and (5).

Thats the gap.

Is an interactive primer the right vehicle?

Yes  for this project, its actually a strength, not a weakness.

Most crypto repos fail because:

  • the README is abstract and hand-wavy, or
  • the code is dense and uncompromising.

Your primer gives:

  • concrete actions,
  • real ciphertext,
  • visible differences between symmetric and anti-symmetric modes.

Thats good.

But interactivity alone does not teach the mental model.

Where the current primer succeeds

1. It shows usage shape clearly

A newcomer learns:

  • keys exist
  • encryption produces a container
  • decryption is not symmetric in all modes
  • authority exists as a concept

Thats already better than most papers.

3. It encourages experimentation

Where it fails (and why)

❌ It doesnt establish why RedX exists

Right now, a newcomer can run the primer and still ask:

Why wouldnt I just use AES-GCM + Ed25519?
